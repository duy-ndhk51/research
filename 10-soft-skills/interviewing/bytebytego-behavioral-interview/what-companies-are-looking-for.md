# What Companies Are Looking For

> **Source**: [ByteByteGo Behavioral Interview: What Companies Are Looking For](https://bytebytego.com/courses/behavioral-interview/what-companies-are-looking-for)  
> **Status**: Summarized  
> **Topic**: fit, leveling, story calibration  
> **Last reviewed**: 2026-06-01

---

## One-Sentence Summary

Companies use behavioral interviews to answer two questions: whether you fit the role and company, and what level your past behavior proves you can operate at.

---

## Core Idea

Technical interviews show whether you can do the technical work. Behavioral interviews show whether you can succeed in a specific environment and at the expected level. Companies infer this through signals in your stories: what situations you choose, what actions you emphasize, how you make decisions, how much scope you affect, and how clearly you explain your personal contribution.

The same experience can send different signals depending on the company. Spending weeks perfecting architecture may signal quality at one company and slow decision-making at another. Shipping quickly and improving later may signal good startup judgment or unacceptable risk in a regulated environment.

---

## Why It Matters

Fit and level determine the offer outcome. A candidate can be rejected despite strong technical skills if their stories suggest poor fit. A candidate can also be down-leveled if their stories do not prove the required scope, contribution, impact, or difficulty for the target level.

The goal is not to manufacture stories. The goal is to select truthful experiences and emphasize the parts that match the company, role, and level.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Role fit asks whether you can handle the specific conditions of the job, such as ambiguity, cross-team coordination, rapid iteration, or operational rigor.
- Company fit asks whether your working style, decision-making, and values match how the organization gets things done.
- Behaviors can be positive in one company and negative in another; context changes the signal.
- Level is assessed through four dimensions: scope, contribution, impact, and difficulty.
- Strong stories need all four dimensions; missing one can lead to down-leveling or rejection.
- Recruiters, job descriptions, engineering blogs, public talks, postmortems, employee discussions, and current employees can reveal what the company actually values.

---

## Mental Model

Behavioral interview performance is the intersection of three things:

1. My real experience.
2. The role and company signals.
3. The target level evidence.

The best story is not always the most technically impressive story. It is the true story that best proves fit and level for the opportunity.

---

## Practical Application

Use this lesson before choosing behavioral stories for a specific company.

Apply it by:

1. Researching what the company and role reward.
2. Choosing stories that match those signals.
3. Calibrating each story against scope, contribution, impact, and difficulty.
4. Leading with two or three stories that clearly operate at the target level.
5. Avoiding exaggeration; interviewers will probe the details.

## Evaluation / Calibration

### Fit Signals

| Tension | What To Calibrate | Risk If Misaligned |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|
| Independence vs. collaboration | Whether the company rewards solo ownership or group alignment | You may look like someone who steamrolls others or someone who cannot make decisions |
| Speed vs. thoroughness | Whether fast iteration or careful validation matters more | You may look slow in a startup or reckless in a regulated domain |
| Excellence vs. pragmatism | Whether the environment rewards perfect architecture or deadline-driven delivery | You may look over-engineered or careless |
| Innovation vs. stability | Whether the role needs new approaches or reliable stewardship | You may look disruptive or uncreative |
| Direct vs. diplomatic | How disagreement and feedback are expected to happen | You may look abrasive or indirect |
| Data vs. intuition | Whether decisions need strong evidence or experienced judgment | You may look under-rigorous or overly slow |
| Specialist vs. generalist | Whether the company needs depth in one area or broad ownership | You may present the wrong shape of capability |

### Level Dimensions

| Dimension | Question | Stronger-Level Signal |
|-----------|----------|-----------------------|
| Scope | Who was affected by the work? | The work affects a whole team, multiple teams, or the organization |
| Contribution | What did I personally do? | Clear ownership, leadership, decision-making, and direction-setting |
| Impact | What changed because of the work? | Quantified technical, user, operational, or business outcomes |
| Difficulty | What made the problem hard? | Ambiguity, constraints, trade-offs, cross-team complexity, or novel problems |

### Level Progression

| Level | Typical Story Shape |
|-------|---------------------|
| Entry | Executes assigned work and owns small pieces that help self or nearby teammates |
| Mid | Owns complete solutions and improves meaningful parts of team effectiveness |
| Senior | Leads team-level initiatives, handles ambiguity, mentors others, and influences adjacent teams |
| Staff | Drives cross-team direction, resolves competing priorities, and creates capabilities multiple teams use |
| Principal | Shapes organization-level systems, strategy, standards, and capabilities across many teams |

---

## Common Mismatches

- Junior stories for senior roles: the story focuses on individual execution without showing team influence or leadership.
- Mid-level stories for staff roles: the story shows single-team ownership but not cross-team strategy or leverage.
- Claiming unearned scope: the candidate says they led a large effort but only owned a small part.
- All impact, no difficulty: the work mattered but was not complex enough to prove the target level.
- All difficulty, no impact: the work was technically hard but did not produce meaningful outcomes.
- Unclear contribution: the story uses "we" so much that the interviewer cannot identify the candidate's role.

---

## Research Checklist

- Ask the recruiter what competencies matter most, what challenges the company is facing, and whether prep materials exist.
- Mine job postings for repeated words such as fast-paced, compliance, ownership, customer, scale, or collaboration.
- Read engineering blogs, public talks, open-source work, documentation, status pages, and postmortems.
- Look for repeated patterns in Glassdoor, Blind, Reddit, or other employee discussions instead of trusting one-off rants.
- Ask current employees what behaviors get rewarded, what behaviors receive negative feedback, and how disagreements are resolved.

---

## Personal Operating Notes

- Before interviews, map each story to fit signals and level dimensions.
- Keep at least two or three stories that are solidly at the target level.
- Emphasize different truthful parts of the same project depending on what the role values.
- Treat mismatch as useful data. The right offer is at the right level in the right environment, not just any offer.

---

## Questions To Revisit

- Which companies reward the way I naturally like to work?
- Which of my stories prove senior-level scope, contribution, impact, and difficulty at the same time?
- Where am I underselling impact because I lack numbers?
- Where am I overstating contribution because the work was really led by someone else?
