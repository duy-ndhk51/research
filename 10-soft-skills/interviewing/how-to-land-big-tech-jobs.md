## TL;DR

A practical playbook for getting recruited by Big Tech companies (FAANG+) through **inbound strategies** — personal branding, networking, and visibility — rather than cold-applying through career portals. The core premise: **make recruiters come to you** by building a strong online presence, optimizing your professional profiles, and strategically networking.

## Key Concepts

### 1. The Problem with Traditional Job Applications

- **Cold applications have abysmal success rates** — most Big Tech companies receive hundreds of thousands of applications; the odds of getting noticed through a portal are extremely low
- **ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems)** filter out most resumes before a human ever sees them
- **Referrals and recruiter outreach** account for the majority of actual hires at top companies
- The traditional "spray and pray" approach wastes time and creates a false sense of progress

### 2. The Inbound Mindset Shift

Instead of *applying to companies*, make companies *come to you*:

| Traditional (Outbound) | Inbound Strategy |
|------------------------|------------------|
| Mass-apply on job portals | Build visible online presence |
| Tailor resume for each role | Optimize LinkedIn to attract recruiters |
| Hope ATS doesn't filter you | Get found through search algorithms |
| Cold emails to recruiters | Warm introductions through network |
| Compete with 1000+ applicants | Enter through referral/recruiter pipeline |

### 3. LinkedIn Optimization — The Foundation

LinkedIn is the **#1 channel** where Big Tech recruiters source candidates. Optimization strategy:

#### Profile Headline
- Don't just use your job title — include **keywords recruiters search for**
- Format: `[Role] | [Key Technologies] | [Domain Expertise]`
- Example: `Senior Software Engineer | React, Node.js, AWS | Building Scalable Web Applications`

#### About Section
- Write in **first person**, conversational tone
- Lead with your **value proposition** — what problems you solve
- Include **keywords naturally** (languages, frameworks, methodologies)
- End with a soft CTA: "Open to discussing interesting opportunities"

#### Experience Section
- Focus on **impact and metrics**, not responsibilities
- Use the **XYZ Formula**: "Accomplished [X] as measured by [Y], by doing [Z]"
- Example: "Reduced API latency by 40% (from 200ms to 120ms) by implementing Redis caching layer and query optimization"

#### Skills & Endorsements
- Add **all relevant technical skills** — recruiters filter by these
- Get endorsements from colleagues (especially for top skills)
- Order skills strategically: most in-demand first

#### Featured Section
- Pin blog posts, talks, open-source contributions
- Visual proof of expertise attracts attention

#### Open to Work Settings
- Enable **"Open to Work"** (visible to recruiters only — not your current employer)
- Select specific roles, locations, and work types
- This dramatically increases your visibility in recruiter searches

### 4. Content Creation & Thought Leadership

Creating content establishes you as an **authority** and creates **passive visibility**:

#### What to Post
- **Technical learnings**: "Today I learned..." about a technology or concept
- **Project case studies**: Problems you solved, with before/after
- **Industry opinions**: Hot takes on tech trends (generates engagement)
- **Career reflections**: Lessons learned, mistakes made
- **Code snippets & tips**: Quick, actionable technical content

#### Posting Strategy
- **Consistency > quality**: Post 2-3 times per week minimum
- **Engage with others**: Comment on posts by engineers at target companies
- **Use hashtags strategically**: #SoftwareEngineering, #WebDevelopment, etc.
- **Tell stories**: Narrative posts outperform dry technical content
- **Hook in the first line**: LinkedIn truncates after ~2 lines — make them count

#### Platform Expansion
- **Twitter/X**: Tech Twitter is where many engineering leads hang out
- **Dev.to / Hashnode / Medium**: Technical blog posts for deep dives
- **GitHub**: Active contribution profile signals engineering commitment
- **YouTube / Podcasts**: If comfortable, video/audio content has high ROI

### 5. Strategic Networking

Networking is **not** collecting contacts — it's building **genuine relationships**:

#### The Target List
1. Identify **10-15 dream companies**
2. Find **engineers and engineering managers** at those companies on LinkedIn
3. Focus on people who:
   - Share content regularly (they're already open to connecting)
   - Work on teams/products you're interested in
   - Are 1-2 levels above your target role

#### The Approach
- **Don't lead with "I want a job"** — lead with value
- **Comment on their content** thoughtfully before connecting
- **Send personalized connection requests** referencing specific content/projects
- **Ask genuine questions** about their work, tech stack, or challenges
- **Offer value first**: share relevant articles, make introductions, give feedback

#### The Coffee Chat
- Ask for 15-20 minute informal conversations
- Prepare **3-5 thoughtful questions** about their experience
- **Listen more than you talk** (80/20 rule)
- Follow up with a thank-you and stay in touch periodically
- **Don't ask for a referral on the first call** — build the relationship first

#### Referral Strategy
- After 2-3 genuine interactions, it's appropriate to mention you're exploring opportunities
- Make it **easy for them to refer you**: send your resume, the specific job posting, and a brief summary of why you're a fit
- A strong referral typically **fast-tracks** you past the resume screen

### 6. GitHub & Open Source Presence

Your GitHub profile is a **portfolio** that speaks louder than resume bullet points:

- **Pin your best repositories** — quality over quantity
- **Write great READMEs** with screenshots, architecture diagrams, and live demos
- **Contribute to popular open-source projects** — even small contributions count
- **Maintain a consistent commit history** — shows ongoing engineering activity
- **Create side projects** that demonstrate skills relevant to target roles

### 7. Resume & Portfolio Optimization

Even with an inbound strategy, your resume still matters once you're in the pipeline:

#### Resume Best Practices
- **One page** (unless 10+ years experience)
- **Quantify everything**: numbers, percentages, dollar amounts
- **Tailor for each company's tech stack** if possible
- **Use active verbs**: Designed, Implemented, Optimized, Led, Reduced, Scaled
- **Education at the bottom** (unless fresh graduate or prestigious school)

#### Portfolio Website (Optional but Powerful)
- Personal domain with: About, Projects, Blog, Contact
- Demonstrates frontend skills while showcasing your work
- Helps with SEO when recruiters Google your name

### 8. Preparing for the Interviews

Once the interviews start coming in, preparation is everything:

#### Technical Preparation
- **Data Structures & Algorithms**: LeetCode (focus on Medium difficulty — that's where most Big Tech interviews sit)
- **System Design**: Practice designing real systems (URL shortener, Twitter feed, chat app)
- **Coding**: Practice in your strongest language; focus on clean, readable code
- **Behavioral**: Prepare 5-7 STAR stories covering leadership, conflict, failure, ambiguity

#### Interview Process at Big Tech (Typical)
1. **Recruiter Screen** (30 min) — background, motivation, role fit
2. **Phone/Technical Screen** (45-60 min) — 1-2 coding problems
3. **Virtual/On-site Loop** (4-6 rounds):
   - 2-3 Coding rounds (algorithms, data structures)
   - 1 System Design round (senior+ roles)
   - 1-2 Behavioral rounds (leadership principles, culture fit)
4. **Hiring Committee Review** — independent review of all feedback
5. **Offer & Negotiation**

#### Study Timeline
| Timeline | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| Months 1-2 | Fundamentals: arrays, strings, hashmaps, trees, graphs |
| Month 3 | Patterns: sliding window, two pointers, BFS/DFS, dynamic programming |
| Month 4 | System design fundamentals: scalability, databases, caching |
| Month 5 | Mock interviews, behavioral prep, company-specific prep |
| Month 6 | Active interviewing, daily practice maintenance |

### 9. The Multiplier Effect

All strategies compound when used together:

```
LinkedIn Optimization → Recruiters find you
     +
Content Creation → Authority & visibility
     +
Strategic Networking → Warm introductions & referrals
     +
GitHub/Portfolio → Proof of skills
     +
Interview Prep → Converting opportunities to offers
     =
Multiple competing offers → Negotiation leverage
```

### 10. Negotiation After Landing Offers

- **Always negotiate** — first offers are rarely the best offers
- **Get competing offers** — this is your strongest leverage
- **Negotiate total compensation**: base, bonus, equity (RSUs), sign-on bonus
- **Use levels.fyi** to understand market rates for your level
- **Be specific**: "Based on my research and competing offers, I'm looking for $X total compensation"
- **Don't reveal your current salary** — anchor on market value

## Key Principles & Mindset

1. **Play the long game** — building a personal brand takes months, not days
2. **Consistency beats intensity** — small daily actions compound over time
3. **Give before you ask** — provide value in every interaction
4. **Quality over quantity** — 10 warm connections > 1000 cold applications
5. **Be authentic** — recruiters and engineers can spot inauthenticity quickly
6. **Treat your career like a product** — market yourself deliberately
7. **Track everything** — keep a spreadsheet of connections, applications, follow-ups
8. **Don't burn bridges** — the tech industry is smaller than you think
9. **Rejection is data** — use it to improve, not as a reason to stop
10. **Multiple offers = leverage** — never stop interviewing until you've signed

## Big Tech Interview Specifics

### Company Culture Keywords

| Company | Key Values | What They Look For |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Google | Googleyness, innovation | Algorithmic thinking, scalability |
| Amazon | Leadership Principles (14) | Customer obsession, ownership, bias for action |
| Meta | Move fast, be bold | Coding speed, system design depth |
| Apple | Attention to detail | Deep technical expertise, product sense |
| Microsoft | Growth mindset | Collaboration, breadth of knowledge |
| Netflix | Freedom & responsibility | Senior-level autonomy, strong opinions |

### Behavioral Interview Framework (STAR+)

```
S - Situation:  Set the context (brief, specific)
T - Task:       What was YOUR responsibility?
A - Action:     What did YOU specifically do? (not the team)
R - Result:     Quantified outcome
+ - Learning:   What would you do differently?
```

Prepare stories for these themes:
- **A time you disagreed with a teammate/manager**
- **Your most complex technical project**
- **A time you failed and what you learned**
- **How you handled ambiguity**
- **A time you mentored or helped someone grow**
- **Your proudest technical achievement**
- **A time you had to make a decision with incomplete data**
